Geopolitics And The Shift To Cyber-Aware Boards

Galina Antova
6–8 minutes

Galina Antova is the Co-Founder of Claroty, cybersecurity company focusing on protecting the world's critical infrastructure.

The disruption of the pandemic has shown CEOs and boards that, through digital transformation, they have an opportunity to make more aggressive plays that distinguish themselves and their organizations as leaders. While technology adoption and innovation have always been hallmarks of leaders, Accenture finds that in the past three years, they have pulled even further ahead, growing revenues five times faster than laggards.

However, with pervasive connectivity of digital components to physical systems and a rise in remote work came increased cyber risk. Ransomware surged, and critical infrastructure organizations have been squarely in attackers’ sites.

The need to approach digital transformation securely became abundantly clear, which meant CEOs and boards also increased their investment in cybersecurity as a competitive advantage. On the whole, the size of CISO’s security teams grew in 2022 compared to 2021. Additionally, 80% of critical infrastructure organizations report that both their IT and operational technology (OT) security budgets increased in 2021, and more than half say their C-suite and board are very involved in cybersecurity decision-making and oversight.

Geopolitical Conflict And Hybrid War

Increased engagement by CEOs and board members in cybersecurity reflects a shift in perspective of cybersecurity as a cost center to a business enabler, which is a good thing. It’s a position that security leaders have been working toward for decades and requires that boards understand technology innovation risks and objectives.

Now, however, geopolitics adds another dimension to risk management and resilience and begs the question: Do CEOs and board members have the required level of cyber-awareness to understand the impact on their current risk posture and how to optimize resources to mitigate risk?

Hybrid war is something we’ve been aware of for years. The current geopolitical environment has brought into sharp focus how brazen cyberattacks can be used as a weapon, particularly against organizations that hold strategic importance due to their economic sway or critical services they provide. The reportedly unsuccessful attacks on high-voltage electrical substations in Ukraine, believed to be done by Russia’s GRU military intelligence agency, using a variation of Sandworm’s Industroyer malware, is just one example.

However, the importance of resilience was already made real by the ransomware attack on Colonial Pipeline. On the heels of the attack, the company reported they had proactively shut down their pipeline out of an abundance of caution, thus precluding their ability to distribute fuel. A lack of visibility and understanding of their level of exposure, along with limited confidence in their ability to mitigate the impact on the OT network, meant they couldn’t continue operations while under attack.

Cyber-Aware Boards

Boardroom discussions are changing as resilience takes center stage. In fact, Gartner expects that, by 2025, 70% of CEOs will mandate a culture of resilience and recommends risk leaders recognize resilience as a strategic imperative to survive a confluence of threats. Resilience of business operations is the primary objective of CISOs. Attacks and impact are inevitable, but how well prepared the organization is to carry on with business depends on the controls that have been built in. This is where cybersecurity as a competitive advantage and differentiator comes into play.

Executives and board members of critical infrastructure organizations are finding they lack the background to deeply understand cyber risk and how to reduce it and are looking for ways to increase their collective cyber awareness. The need is urgent, and the gap is significant given that a majority of board members are former CEOs and CFOs, with most new directors still coming from those backgrounds (26% and 23%, respectively). The following initiatives can help boards become cyber-aware.

• Eliminate board recruiting barriers to close the gap. Most boards require prior board experience. The practice means that there is significant overlap between boards of the top 50 largest companies in the U.S., leaving little room for diversity and an infusion of expertise needed to close the cyber-awareness gap. Given the dearth of technologists on boards, much less security experts, that requirement needs to be lifted in order to create a seat at the table for CISOs and CIOs.

• CEOs also have to play a new role. Acknowledging cybersecurity projects to enable business objectives, CEOs need to garner support from the rest of the executive team and board to prioritize and accelerate security projects, even if that means some temporary disruptions to the business. The long-term benefit versus the short-term pain is what will allow teams to augment their digital infrastructures quickly. The objective of the CISO is not to eliminate attacks (which is impossible) but to strengthen operational resilience while enabling connectivity and digital transformation to leapfrog the competition.

• In the meantime, learn from your in-house experts. Given all that CISOs have been through in the past few years, boards should make a practice of inviting the CISOs in for regular presentations. Beyond running through risk metrics dashboards, they can lead conversations about what resilient operations look like from a cyber and technology perspective. Successful CISOs have the experience to elevate discussions and share business-critical learnings.

• Establish a dedicated cybersecurity committee. To ensure that cybersecurity gets the appropriate level of attention, Gartner has observed that many boards are forming dedicated cybersecurity committees and predicts that 40% of boards will have these committees in place by 2025. Cybersecurity-related risk is a top concern, so boards need to know they have the proper oversight in place. Organizations need a technologist or cybersecurity professional on the board to lead such a committee.

The companies that win and are successful in the future will be those that disrupt themselves and transform themselves into technology companies to unlock value for their customers. Cyber resilience is a competitive advantage and needs to be discussed at the highest levels of management: boards of directors.
