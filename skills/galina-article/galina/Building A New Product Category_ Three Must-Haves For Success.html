Building A New Product Category: Three Must-Haves For Success

Galina Antova
6–7 minutes

Getty

We live in an age of unprecedented transformation, and the digital world is becoming intimately intertwined with our physical reality. In this chaos lies the opportunity to create new product categories that can further accelerate our pace of digitalization and progress.

The opportunities are immense, yet the vast majority of employees in developed countries stick to jobs that are very traditional and do not offer genuine fulfillment. A poll by Gallup shows that 66% of the U.S. workforce is not fully engaged in their jobs, with the percentage rising to 85% for the global workforce.

As our basic needs continue to be met more easily, humans will inevitably look for higher levels of fulfillment that meaningful work creates. And there has never been a better time to focus our energy on building new ideas, given all the foundational and infrastructure resources we have at our disposal. The infrastructure as a service (IaaS) market, valued at $15.2 billion in 2016, is projected to reach $182.27 billion by 2025. Anyone with a computer and an idea can set up a business poised to scale, reliably and securely, as they grow.

Ten or 15 years ago, starting a new company meant you had to bear the overhead of researching and operationalizing complex systems. Today, there are many creative startups that have focused on taking the pain away, significantly streamlining areas such as finance, HR, travel and others so you can focus on your core value proposition.

One of the most meaningful and rewarding experiences for me has been to help create a new product category and, along the way, a new industry segment. Below, I’ve tried to distill the three processes that need to be completed in order to have a fighting chance of success.

1. Identify the changing assumptions.

Every industry has a set of rules on which it’s built. In my case, in the industrial control systems (ICS) industry, it was the fact that most operational technology (OT) networks were isolated from corporate networks, and there was no need for connectivity. When that reality started to shift about 15 years ago for productivity reasons, we saw a discrepancy between how the OT teams thought of themselves -- disconnected from the rest of the network -- and the reality of multiple connections that OT teams simply were not aware of. So, the de-facto assumptions changed, and there was an opportunity for a new product category to satisfy this new demand and new reality.

The takeaway here is that when you see a major change that alters the underlying assumptions of the industry you’re focused on, a window of opportunity is opening.

2. Identify the underlying need versus the requirement that is being communicated.

There is a discrepancy between what customers say they need and what they show by their actions that they want. As the adage goes, if Henry Ford had asked customers what they wanted, they would’ve said a better horse. Customers can only describe what they want using existing references, but if the incremental product isn’t being used or doesn’t fit, you know you have an opportunity for a new category.

In my case, customers were saying they wanted IT security for their OT networks. However, the IT security products were, at best, ineffective in OT environments because they were not able to parse OT proprietary protocols -- and, more commonly, they were just plain dangerous. Some were crushing network performance, thereby impacting the No. 1 thing they were meant to protect: uptime and availability of the underlying processes. So, we had to create a new product from scratch -- one that delivered the benefits of IT security tools but also respected the requirements of OT.

In other words, listen for what is not being said. Customers will describe the world in their current reality. You must envision the new reality.

3. Carve out a niche for yourself, and dominate it.

As new requirements start to emerge, it’s very common to see large incumbents in your overall industry come up with marketing messaging around a “newer” offering, yet not really deliver anything significantly new. Large companies are not great at innovation, so when their sales and marketing teams sense a new, significant demand in the marketplace, the first thing they adjust is the marketing collateral, without much real change in the technology.

Those shifts are very telling: They mean the companies realize customers are attracted to a new value proposition, but they can’t quite deliver on that yet. This further validates the opportunity and opens the door for you to quickly execute on your idea. In my case, this meant establishing OT cybersecurity as very distinct from the well-established IT cybersecurity domain and focusing on technology that was OT-specific.

The key is to establish a nice subindustry for yourself and actively evangelize it. The new product you create that fits this subcategory will be narrower in scope, but it will perfectly fulfill the new requirements, thereby allowing you to position your company as a truly new and differentiated entrant. For more inspiration on this topic, I highly recommend Geoffrey Moore’s Crossing the Chasm.

Creating a new product category is probably the most challenging aspect of entrepreneurship, but it’s also the most rewarding, in my experience. The fulfillment and personal actualization that comes from it, not to mention the benefit to society, is truly a unique feeling, and I personally believe it has the potential to bring happiness in a very meaningful way.
